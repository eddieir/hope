Extra info & support

Withdrawal timeline

  • 20 minutes: Heart rate and blood pressure start to drop.
  • Day 1: Carbon monoxide clears from your blood; oxygen levels normalize.
  • Day 2–3: Nicotine is fully out of your body. Cravings and irritability usually peak here.
  • Week 1–2: Coughing may increase temporarily as lungs clear out mucus — this is a good sign.
  • Week 2–4: Circulation and lung function keep improving; cravings become shorter and less frequent.
  • 1–3 months: Energy and stamina noticeably better.
  • 9 months+: Coughing, congestion, and shortness of breath continue to decrease.

Handling a craving — the 4 Ds

  • Delay — cravings usually pass in 3–5 minutes. Wait it out.
  • Deep breathe — slow breathing calms the urge and mimics the inhale ritual.
  • Drink water — sip slowly, it gives your hands and mouth something to do.
  • Do something else — walk, text a friend, chew gum — break the association.

Hand-rolled vs. manufactured cigarettes

If you roll your own, a few things are worth knowing:

  • Roll-your-own cigarettes are often smoked without an effective filter, so tar and smoke exposure per cigarette can be as high or higher than filtered manufactured cigarettes.
  • People tend to underestimate how much tobacco they use per roll-up, which can understate real nicotine intake.
  • Because RYO is usually cheaper per gram, the financial savings from quitting build up more slowly than the "per pack" savings a cigarette smoker sees — the health benefits, however, are identical.
  • Nicotine replacement (patches, gum, lozenges) works the same regardless of how you smoked, and is worth considering for either group.

If you slip

A slip is not a restart. Log it, note what triggered it, and get back to your plan the next hour — not "tomorrow." Most people who successfully quit had at least one slip along the way.
